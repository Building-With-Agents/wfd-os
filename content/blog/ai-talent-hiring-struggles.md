Every mid-market company I talk to has the same complaint: "We can't find AI talent."

They post roles on LinkedIn. They work with recruiters. They offer competitive salaries. And they get ghosted by candidates who have three other offers from companies with bigger names, bigger budgets, and bigger engineering teams.

Here's what nobody tells you: **the talent exists. The strategy doesn't.**

## The Real Problem Isn't Supply

There are more people with AI and machine learning skills today than at any point in history. Universities are graduating thousands of data science and CS students every year. Bootcamps are churning out Python developers who can build with LLMs. The supply side has never been stronger.

The problem is that mid-market companies are competing for the same narrow band of "senior AI engineer with 5+ years of production ML experience" candidates that Google, Microsoft, and Amazon are also pursuing. That's a fight you will lose every time.

## What Smart Mid-Market Companies Are Doing Instead

The companies that are successfully building AI capabilities right now share three characteristics:

### 1. They're Building, Not Hiring

Instead of searching for the unicorn AI engineer who can architect, build, and deploy an entire ML pipeline solo, they're engaging firms that build production AI systems with supervised teams. The cost of a 12-week engagement to build a working system ($20-35K) is less than two months of salary for a senior AI engineer you'll spend six months trying to hire.

At Computing for All, we've seen this pattern repeatedly. Workforce Solutions Borderplex needed real-time labor market intelligence. Instead of trying to hire an AI team, they engaged us to build a 6-agent pipeline. Twelve weeks later, they had a production system processing thousands of job listings weekly. Total cost: $25,500. Total time recruiting: zero.

### 2. They're Focusing on the Problem, Not the Technology

The companies that succeed with AI don't start with "we need machine learning." They start with "our team spends 40 hours a month on this manual process and it's killing us."

When you frame the problem operationally, the solution often doesn't require a PhD in computer science. It requires someone who understands your data, your workflow, and how to connect an LLM to your existing systems. That's a much more findable skill set than "senior ML engineer."

### 3. They're Evaluating Talent Through Work, Not Resumes

The resume-based hiring process is particularly broken for AI roles. Candidates list "Python" and "TensorFlow" as skills, but you can't tell from a resume whether they've built a production pipeline or just completed a Coursera course.

The try-before-you-hire model works better: engage a team to build something real, evaluate their work over 10-14 weeks, and then offer to hire the people who performed well. You've seen their actual output in your codebase, with your data, on your timeline. That's a hiring signal that no interview process can match.

## The Math That Changes the Conversation

Consider the true cost of the traditional approach:

- 6 months to find and hire a senior AI engineer: **$180K+ salary**
- Plus recruiting fees (15-25%): **$27-45K**
- Plus 3-6 months of ramp-up before production value
- Plus the risk of a bad hire

Compare that to:

- 12-week engagement to build a production system: **$20-35K**
- Working system in production by week 12
- Option to hire the team members who built it
- Zero recruiting risk — you've already seen their work

The math isn't close.

## What This Means for Your Organization

If you're a mid-market company struggling to build AI capabilities, stop trying to out-recruit Big Tech. Instead:

1. **Identify your highest-impact manual process** — the one consuming the most staff hours or producing the most decision-making latency
2. **Scope it as a project, not a hire** — what would a working solution look like in 12 weeks?
3. **Engage a team that builds with supervised apprentices** — you get production output AND a built-in talent pipeline
4. **Evaluate through work** — hire the people who proved themselves on your project

The AI talent shortage isn't about a lack of people. It's about a lack of imagination in how we find and develop them.
